How Exhibit works

Where each number comes from · how we classify schools · how we refresh · how we handle data anomalies.
Last synced: June 8, 2026 · ABA Standard 509 disclosure cycle 2025 (the most recent published cycle) · BLS OEWS 2024 · BEA RPP 2024 · HUD SAFMR 2025.

Coverage

All ABA law schools. The dataset holds 208 schools: every one of the 197 currently ABA-accredited U.S. law schools, plus 11 recently closed or transitioning schools kept with their full historical record. A ranked table only lists the schools that report the metric it ranks on in the latest cycle, so a ranking can show fewer than 208 — for example 196 schools carry a reported resident-tuition figure. The total never changes meaning: 208 in the dataset, with a subset shown wherever a specific field is required.

Safety · Target · Reach classification

For each school we use its published 2025 LSAT and uGPA 25th and 75th percentile bands (the "middle 50%" of last year's entering class). When you enter your stats:

  • Safety, your LSAT and uGPA are at or above that school's 75th percentile.
  • Target, your stats fall inside the school's middle 50% on both metrics.
  • Reach, your LSAT or uGPA falls below the school's 25th percentile.

The single helper is fitClass(school, lsat, gpa). There is no second weighting, no adjustment for "soft factors," and no proprietary score, the rule above is the entire rule. If you enter only one stat we apply the rule to that one stat alone.

If a school has not published a 25/75 (rare, happens on new accreditations), it's excluded from your Safety/Target/Reach results until the next disclosure cycle.

Cost-of-attendance + loan math

The Net Price calculator uses each school's resident sticker tuition if your selected home state matches the school's state; otherwise the non-resident figure. We subtract the school's median grant amount (from the same 509 disclosure) when no fitClass-based estimate is available; with fitClass we use the band: Safety → school's 75th-pct grant, Target → median grant, Reach → 25th-pct grant.

Loan amortization uses the standard fixed-rate formula P · r · (1+r)n / ((1+r)n – 1) over your selected term. The 3-year living cost is (housing + other) × 9 academic months × 3 years. These are illustrative. Important: under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, students entering from July 1, 2026 can no longer use Grad PLUS, and federal graduate borrowing is capped at $50,000/year ($200,000 lifetime). Any cost above that cap must come from private loans (typically higher rates, and no PSLF or income-driven repayment), so the “true cost” for 2026+ entrants can be materially higher than a pure federal-borrowing model implies. Confirm current federal and private loan rates with your lender before relying on the total cost number.

Why a school's median grant can exceed its resident tuition. At public schools the 509 grant figures (25th / median / 75th-pct) are reported as a single school-wide distribution — residents and non-residents pooled — and scholarships are typically awarded against the higher non-resident sticker. So at a school like Nebraska the median grant (~$20,000) sits above the resident tuition (~$13,200) yet below the non-resident figure (~$35,600), and the 75th-pct grant (~$35,100) lands right at the non-resident line. This is an artifact of residency-pooled, sticker-scaled aid reporting, not a data error; the resident-tuition series itself is unaffected.

Bar passage

The headline First-Time Bar figure is the ABA-published first-time pass rate for the most recent reporting year (currently 2025, describing the 2024 graduating cohort). The 2-Yr Ultimate figure is a separate ABA disclosure with a longer measurement window, it captures graduates who passed within two years of graduation. The two figures are not directly comparable across cohorts.

Importantly: a school's bar on any given row describes graduates who entered roughly three years earlier, while the apps/offers/acceptance figures on the same row describe the most recent entering class. Treat the bar number as evidence about the school three years ago.

Employment outcomes

FTLT Employment = "Full-Time, Long-Term, JD-Required or JD-Advantage" employment 10 months after graduation, per the school's ABA 509 outcome report. We display it as both a percent of the graduating class and an estimated graduate count (graduates × pct / 100).

The 10-category employment breakdown (MegaLaw 500+ / BigLaw 251-500 / Mid 101-250 / Small 2-100 / Clerkships / Public Interest / Government / Business / Academia / Solo / Seeking) is reported by the school in the same disclosure. We rank rows by applicant-mental-model desirability rather than alphabetically. The "vs national average" delta on each row compares the school's percent against the unweighted average across all 197 reporting schools.

Adjusted ("real-world") employment

The headline FTLT rate counts law-school/university-funded positions — short-term fellowships the school itself pays for — the same as market jobs. The ABA discloses these separately, so we surface a second figure that strips them out:

Real-world FTLT % = (Total FTLT − Funded FTLT) ÷ graduates × 100, where Funded FTLT = the school-funded full-time, long-term Bar-Passage-Required and JD-Advantage positions from the school's own ABA Employment Summary. We show the reported rate, the funded share, and the adjusted rate side by side, plus the gap over time. For most schools the two are nearly identical; where a school leans on funded roles the difference is the point. This is the school's own reported funded count — we subtract, we do not estimate.

Splitter lean (LSAT vs GPA)

A splitter has a high LSAT and a lower GPA; a reverse splitter the opposite. We surface a per-school lean: the national percentile rank of the school's median LSAT minus the national percentile rank of its median GPA, for the same cycle. A positive lean means the LSAT is the more selective of the two numbers (the class skews splitter); negative means the GPA is (skews reverse). We bucket +12 or more as splitter-leaning and −12 or less as reverse, and trend the lean across all 15 years so you can see whether the tilt is consistent.

Important limitation, stated plainly. The ABA publishes LSAT and GPA as separate (marginal) distributions — never the joint distribution of who was admitted. So this metric cannot measure a splitter admit rate, and we never claim it does. It measures what a school's class numbers weight, which is a strong, defensible proxy for application strategy — but it is an inference from marginal data, not a count of admitted splitters. Treat the lean and the splitter index as a starting hypothesis to pressure-test, not a guarantee.

Conditional-scholarship attrition (the "risk factor")

A conditional scholarship requires you to hold a class-rank or GPA threshold to keep it past year one. The ABA reports, per year, how many students entered with one and how many had it reduced or eliminated. We compute the stripped rate over the most recent reporting window (up to 5 years): Σ eliminated ÷ Σ entered × 100, and flag it as a risk factor — moderate at ≥15%, high at ≥33%. Because only a fixed slice of any class can finish above the school's own curve, a structurally high strip rate is a budgeting warning, not a moral judgment of the school; we frame it as a number to plan around.

Trend charts + the 15-year history

Every line chart on the site is built from year-by-year ABA 509 disclosures back to 2011. Where a metric was not collected in a given year (most notably the bar pass cadence change 2012-2017), the chart renders a dashed bridge through the missing years with hollow markers labeled "YYYY not reported (interpolated)". The interpolation is for visual continuity only, we never replace null values with synthetic numbers anywhere a real cell exists.

Data quality + outlier handling

Two independent compilations stand behind the data, and what Exhibit displays is their reconciliation. The first is a hand-built master workbook of the ABA Standard 509 disclosures, spot-checked against the source PDFs since 2011 — about 2.44 million non-null data points (2,437,481) across its 14 section sheets, spanning 218 school identifiers at roughly 12,200 facts per full-history school (median 12,178; range 75–12,821; the two widest sheets, Attrition and JD Enrollment & Ethnicity, alone hold ~1.4 million of them). The second is an independent primary-source re-derivation built straight from the original ABA workbooks, cell by cell. Across ~28,800 overlapping headline cells — LSAT and uGPA percentiles, acceptance, applications, offers, enrollment, first-time bar, takers and passers, and tuition — the two compilations agree on 99.86%. The remaining 0.14% is concentrated in a single adjudicated identity case (the Rutgers Camden/Newark split) and is documented cell-by-cell rather than silently resolved.

How big is the dataset, exactly

Total: about 2.44 million data points — 2,437,481 non-null facts across the 14 data sheets (excluding the Dashboard, the hidden list, and the spine columns like School/Year/Status). Two sheets dominate because of their wide demographic column sets: Attrition (866K, now including the computed gender-Other residuals) and JD Enrollment & Ethnicity (534K), followed by Employment (467K). Everything else is under ~125K each.

Per school: ~12,200 for a typical full-history school. There are 218 distinct school identifiers in the set, with a median of 12,178 facts each and a range from 75 to 12,821. The mean (11,181) sits a bit lower because it's dragged down by a dozen-plus small rows — and that's the texture worth knowing:

The heavy hitters are the elite full-history schools — Georgetown (12,821), George Washington (12,678), Harvard (12,640), UVA (12,575), Duke (12,573) — all carrying the full 2011–2026 span across every metric. The thin ones are exactly what they should be: the X-flagged Cooley campus components (153 each) and predecessor lines, which by design hold only the years they existed and never get summed.

Sanitization rules applied at inline time

  • Negative scholarship counts in Historic (a known reporting artifact in 2015 and 2024 for ~10 schools) are nulled, not interpolated.
  • Bar pass rates reported as fractions instead of percents are auto-normalized to %.
  • Tuition values that are impossible as an annual sticker, a stored $0 or a truncated single-term fragment (e.g. $35, $120, $1,094), are shown as an open circle ("not reported") rather than plotted as a false near-zero; the raw gz layer keeps the original source value. Implausible single-year spikes or halvings are corrected against the source where they can be adjudicated (and ringed with a hover note), or carry an "under review" flag where they can't (see below).
  • Per-credit-only schools (~40 schools, across the 2011–2025 history) report tuition by the credit hour, not as an annual sticker; we convert to annual using ≈ 30 credits per full-time academic year (the standard JD pacing, calibrated against each school's own adjacent annual years to a median ~5%). Earlier these years showed a $0 gap; they now carry the converted figure, with the per-credit conversion noted here.

Every transformation is reversible from the original public disclosure. The methodology is public; the input files are public; the transformations are documented above.

Tuition anomalies: corrected, ringed, and logged

When a cell is a provable reporting error — a $0/text-marker corruption, a per-term value at half or a third of the annual figure, a doubled cell, an inverted percentile, a stale sticker after a price cut — we correct it against the disclosure and the school's own adjacent years. Every correction shows on the chart as an amber ring with a hover note and is logged old → new in our corrections log. The raw layer keeps the original value, so each edit is reversible. We don't change a value we can't adjudicate.

  • Not reported. A $0 or truncated cell with nothing trustworthy to recover renders as an open circle, not a false near-zero. Where a reliable figure exists, we recover it.
  • Genuine resets. A real price cut or per-credit restructuring is kept as-is; a large-but-plausible one-year move carries an “under review” note rather than an overwrite.

4-year projections

Trend charts extend the last reported year with a dashed 4-year projection and a shaded uncertainty cone. This is a model output, not a forecast of fact, it answers "if this school's own recent trend simply continued, where would it land?" and nothing more. A school can break its trend at any time; treat the dashed region as a sketch, not a promise.

Method (a recency-weighted fit to each school's most recent 5 reported years, with the latest years weighted most, so the projection follows where the school is heading now without swinging on a single year, and never the national average):

  • Rates bounded 0–100% (bar pass, employment shares, acceptance): a linear fit on the log-odds (logistic), so projections stay inside 0–100% and slow as they approach a ceiling/floor rather than shooting past it.
  • Money and counts (tuition, applications, enrollment): a log-linear fit, i.e. a constant compound annual growth rate.
  • LSAT / uGPA and everything else: ordinary least-squares linear regression.
  • The cone is the regression's prediction standard error, widening with distance from the data (softened ×1.4), inverse-transformed back to the metric's own scale.
  • We only project when a school has at least 3 reported years for that metric; otherwise no dashed line is drawn.

The math is deliberately simple and transparent so anyone can reproduce or challenge it. It does not model policy changes, the economy, or a school's strategy, only its recent numerical trajectory.

Refresh cadence

The ABA publishes Standard 509 disclosures once per year, typically in the December–February window for the prior calendar year's data. We re-sync within the month following each ABA release. The "Last synced" date at the top of this page and in the disclaimer bar always reflects the most recent inline pull.

Wage (BLS), cost-of-living (BEA), and rent (HUD) feeds follow their own annual cadences, we re-pull each within a month of the respective agency's release.

What Exhibit does NOT do

  • We do not survey students or schools. Every number is from a published government or ABA source.
  • We do not rank, score, or recommend schools.
  • We do not estimate fields the underlying source did not publish. Where a cell is missing, the UI shows —.
  • We are not affiliated with the American Bar Association or with any law school.
